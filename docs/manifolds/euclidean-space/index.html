1.1 Euclidean Space

A special vector space equipped with distance function.

1.1.1 Vector Space​

Note for vectors X,Y∈RnX, Y \in \mathbb{R}^nX,Y∈Rn we could perform vector addition and scalar multiplication, namely:

note

In this textbook, we use notations below to represents a vector: X∈Rn=(X1,X2,…,Xn)X \in \mathbb{R}^n = (X^1,X^2,\dots,X^n)X∈Rn=(X1,X2,…,Xn)

X+Y=(X1+Y1,X2+Y2,…,Xn+Yn)X+Y = (X^1+Y^1,X^2+Y^2,\dots,X^n+Y^n)X+Y=(X1+Y1,X2+Y2,…,Xn+Yn)
aX=(aX1,aX2,…,aXn)aX = (aX^1, aX^2, \dots, aX^n)aX=(aX1,aX2,…,aXn)

If for all vectors X,Y,Z∈VX,Y,Z\in\mathbb{V}X,Y,Z∈V and for all scalars a,b∈Fa,b \in \mathbb{F}a,b∈F, the following eight equations stand, we say V\mathbb{V}V is a vector space over field F\mathbb{F}F:

  • Associative: (X+Y)+Z=X+(Y+Z)(X+Y)+Z = X+(Y+Z)(X+Y)+Z=X+(Y+Z)

  • Multiplication Identity: 1X=X\mathbf{1}X = X1X=X

1.1.2 Inner Product​

Inner product, also called the Euclidean inner product is defined as following:

If X=(X1,X2,…,Xn),Y=(Y1,Y2,…,Yn),X⋅Y=∑i=1nXiYi\text{If }X=(X^1,X^2,\dots,X^n),Y=(Y^1,Y^2,\dots,Y^n),X\cdot Y=\sum_{i=1}^nX^iY^iIf X=(X1,X2,…,Xn),Y=(Y1,Y2,…,Yn),X⋅Y=i=1∑n​XiYi

where X⋅YX\cdot YX⋅Yis the inner product.

1.1.3 Inner Product Space​

Assume we have a vector space V\mathbb{V}V over a field F\mathbb{F}F, if for all a,b∈Fa,b \in \mathbb{F}a,b∈F and for all X,Y,Z∈VX,Y,Z \in \mathbb{V}X,Y,Z∈V, W∈V−{0}W \in \mathbb{V}-\{\mathbf{0}\}W∈V−{0} we have the following statements satisfied, then we say (V,⋅)(\mathbb{V},\cdot)(V,⋅) is a inner product space over field F\mathbb{F}F, where ⋅\cdot⋅ is the inner product,

  • Well-defined: X⋅Y∈FX\cdot Y \in \mathbb{F}X⋅Y∈F
  • Symmetry: X⋅Y=Y⋅XX\cdot Y = Y\cdot XX⋅Y=Y⋅X
  • Bilinearity: (aX+bY)⋅Z=a(X⋅Y)+b(Y⋅Z)(aX + bY)\cdot Z = a(X \cdot Y)+b(Y\cdot Z)(aX+bY)⋅Z=a(X⋅Y)+b(Y⋅Z)
  • Positive-definite: W⋅W>0W\cdot W > 0W⋅W>0

1.1.4 Euclidean Space​

An Euclidean vector space is a finite-dimensional inner product space over the real numbers.

1.1.5 Euclidean Distance and Angles​

Assume V⊂Rn\mathbb{V} \subset \mathbb{R}^nV⊂Rn is an euclidean space and we have X,Y,Z∈VX,Y,Z\in \mathbb{V}X,Y,Z∈V.

Then the length of XXX is defined by:

∥X∥:=X⋅X\lVert X \rVert := \sqrt{X \cdot X}∥X∥:=X⋅X​

the distance between XXX and YYY is defined by:

d(X,Y):=∥X−Y∥d(X,Y) := \lVert X-Y\rVertd(X,Y):=∥X−Y∥
Properties
  • Symmetry: d(X,Y)=d(Y,X)d(X,Y) = d(Y,X)d(X,Y)=d(Y,X)
  • Positive definite: d(X,Y)≥0d(X,Y) \geq 0d(X,Y)≥0, and d(X,Y)=0  ⟺  X=Yd(X,Y) = 0 \iff X=Yd(X,Y)=0⟺X=Y.
  • Triangle inequality: d(X,Z)≤d(X,Y)+d(Y,Z)d(X,Z) \leq d(X,Y) + d(Y,Z)d(X,Z)≤d(X,Y)+d(Y,Z)
